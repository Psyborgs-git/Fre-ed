## Band construction

A **Bollinger Band** is a volatility envelope plotted around a simple moving average. John Bollinger introduced the concept in the 1980s as a way to define "high" and "low" relative to recent price history — not in absolute terms, but in terms of the current *volatility regime*.

The scene shows the price line (cyan) between amber upper and lower bands. A violet centre line is the SMA. Red spheres appear when price touches the upper band; green spheres at the lower band. Adjust Period to change the SMA window and StdDev to widen or narrow the envelope.

The three components are:

$$
\text{SMA}_n(t) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} P_{t-k}
$$

$$
\sigma_n(t) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \bigl(P_{t-k} - \text{SMA}_n(t)\bigr)^2}
$$

$$
\text{Upper}_t = \text{SMA}_n(t) + m \cdot \sigma_n(t), \quad \text{Lower}_t = \text{SMA}_n(t) - m \cdot \sigma_n(t)
$$

where $n$ is the period (default 20) and $m$ is the multiplier (default 2.0). Bollinger himself found that roughly **90% of price action occurs within the bands** at the default settings.

<CodeBlock language="python">{`import pandas as pd
import numpy as np

def bollinger_bands(prices: pd.Series,
                    period: int = 20,
                    std_dev: float = 2.0) -> pd.DataFrame:
    """Compute Bollinger Bands. Returns DataFrame with sma, upper, lower."""
    sma   = prices.rolling(period).mean()
    sigma = prices.rolling(period).std(ddof=0)  # population std
    upper = sma + std_dev * sigma
    lower = sma - std_dev * sigma
    return pd.DataFrame({"sma": sma, "upper": upper, "lower": lower,
                         "bandwidth": (upper - lower) / sma,
                         "pct_b": (prices - lower) / (upper - lower)})

# Example
import yfinance as yf
prices = yf.download("SPY", start="2023-01-01", end="2024-01-01")["Close"]
bands  = bollinger_bands(prices, period=20, std_dev=2.0)
print(bands.tail())
`}</CodeBlock>

## Squeeze and expansion

The most powerful Bollinger Band signal is not the touch of a band — it is the *width* of the bands itself. **Bandwidth** is:

$$
\text{BW}_t = \frac{\text{Upper}_t - \text{Lower}_t}{\text{SMA}_t}
$$

When bandwidth falls to a multi-year low, it signals a **squeeze**: volatility has contracted and a large directional move is imminent (though direction is unknown). When bandwidth expands sharply, the move has begun.

The **%B indicator** measures where price sits within the bands:

$$
\%B_t = \frac{P_t - \text{Lower}_t}{\text{Upper}_t - \text{Lower}_t}
$$

- $\%B = 1.0$: price is exactly at the upper band
- $\%B = 0.5$: price is at the SMA
- $\%B = 0.0$: price is exactly at the lower band
- $\%B > 1.0$ or $\%B < 0.0$: price has broken outside the band

In the scene, the squeeze period is visible between indices 20–30: the amber bands narrow visibly. Lower StdDev multiplier on the slider reveals the squeeze effect more dramatically.

### Bollinger Band Width indicator

<CodeBlock language="python">{`def detect_squeeze(bands: pd.DataFrame,
                   lookback: int = 120,
                   threshold_pct: float = 0.05) -> pd.Series:
    """Return True at bars where bandwidth is in the bottom threshold_pct
    of its lookback window — i.e., a Bollinger squeeze."""
    bw_min = bands["bandwidth"].rolling(lookback).min()
    bw_max = bands["bandwidth"].rolling(lookback).max()
    bw_range = bw_max - bw_min
    # normalise bandwidth within its lookback range
    bw_rank = (bands["bandwidth"] - bw_min) / bw_range
    return bw_rank < threshold_pct

# Example
squeeze = detect_squeeze(bands)
print("Squeeze bars:", squeeze.sum())
`}</CodeBlock>

## Mean-reversion signals

The classic **mean-reversion** interpretation of Bollinger Bands assumes the price is range-bound:

- **Buy** when price closes *below* the lower band (oversold).
- **Sell short** when price closes *above* the upper band (overbought).
- **Exit** when price returns to the SMA.

This works well in **low-trending, high-mean-reverting** conditions (e.g., equity indices during low-volatility sideways regimes).

$$
\text{Signal}^{\text{MR}}_t = \begin{cases}
+1 & P_t \leq \text{Lower}_t \\
-1 & P_t \geq \text{Upper}_t \\
\phantom{+}0 & |{}\%B_t - 0.5| < 0.1 \text{ (exit zone)}
\end{cases}
$$

<Callout type="warning">
Never trade mean-reversion signals in a strong trend without a trend filter. Repeatedly fading an uptrending breakout is one of the most reliable ways to blow up an account. Add a 200-day SMA filter: only take long mean-reversion signals when price is above the 200-day SMA.
</Callout>

## Breakout signals

During and after a squeeze, Bollinger Bands switch from a **mean-reversion** tool to a **breakout** tool:

- **Breakout long**: first close *above* the upper band after a prolonged squeeze — the price is breaking out of its volatility compression.
- **Breakout short**: first close *below* the lower band after a squeeze.

The key heuristic: **trend-follow the first touch of the band after a squeeze; mean-revert the repeated touches during a range**.

<CodeBlock language="python">{`def bollinger_signals(prices: pd.Series,
                      period: int = 20,
                      std_dev: float = 2.0,
                      squeeze_lookback: int = 60) -> pd.DataFrame:
    """
    Combine squeeze detection with band-touch signals.
    Returns DataFrame with columns: signal, in_squeeze, pct_b.
    """
    bands   = bollinger_bands(prices, period, std_dev)
    squeeze = detect_squeeze(bands, lookback=squeeze_lookback)

    signal = pd.Series(0, index=prices.index)
    for i in range(1, len(prices)):
        pct_b = bands["pct_b"].iloc[i]
        was_squeezed = squeeze.iloc[i - 1]

        if was_squeezed:
            # Breakout mode after squeeze
            if pct_b > 1.0:
                signal.iloc[i] = 1    # breakout long
            elif pct_b < 0.0:
                signal.iloc[i] = -1   # breakout short
        else:
            # Mean-reversion mode during range
            if pct_b < 0.05:
                signal.iloc[i] = 1    # oversold: long
            elif pct_b > 0.95:
                signal.iloc[i] = -1   # overbought: short

    return pd.DataFrame({
        "signal":     signal,
        "in_squeeze": squeeze,
        "pct_b":      bands["pct_b"],
        "bandwidth":  bands["bandwidth"],
    })

# Backtest-ready signal generation
signals = bollinger_signals(prices, period=20, std_dev=2.0)
print(signals[signals["signal"] != 0].head(10))
`}</CodeBlock>

### Volatility-based position sizing

Because band width directly measures volatility, it provides a natural **position-sizing input**:

$$
\text{Shares}_t = \frac{\text{Risk budget}}{\text{BW}_t \cdot P_t}
$$

When the bands are wide (high volatility), position size shrinks automatically. When the bands are narrow (low volatility pre-squeeze), the full position is deployed. This is a form of **volatility targeting** — a technique also used in risk-parity and trend-following strategies.
